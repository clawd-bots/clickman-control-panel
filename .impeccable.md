## Design Context

### Users
Small performance marketing team (2-5 people) at AndYou.ph, a Philippine telehealth company selling weight loss, hair regrowth, and men's health products. Primary user is the media buyer (Jordan), who checks this throughout the day during ad optimization sessions. A manager and CEO glance at it weekly for high-level numbers. The dashboard is the team's command center — opened first thing in the morning, referenced during campaign adjustments, reviewed in team syncs.

### Brand Personality
**Sharp. Tactical. Relentless.**

Click-Man is a weapon for performance marketers, not a pretty reporting tool. Every element earns its place. No fluff, no decorative filler. The name "Click-Man" is a media buyer's alter ego — the person who turns clicks into revenue. The interface should feel like suiting up for work.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Primary theme:** Dark mode. This is where the dashboard lives. Light mode exists as an option but dark is home.
- **Reference:** Triple Whale — familiar data density, similar metric patterns, proven UX for DTC marketers. Also Klar analytics for the clean dark aesthetic.
- **Signature element:** Liquid glass — frosted translucent surfaces, subtle backdrop blur, layered depth. Think Apple visionOS / iOS 26 glassmorphism: cards that feel like they float on the dark background with soft light refraction at edges. Not overdone — use it for key surfaces (cards, panels, dropdowns, modals) while keeping data text crisp and readable. The glass effect should reinforce the "premium control center" feel.
- **Anti-references:** Generic SaaS dashboards with pastel cards. Anything that looks like a Figma template. Overly minimal designs that sacrifice data density for whitespace. Cheap glassmorphism with heavy blur and neon borders — the glass should be subtle and refined, not a nightclub.
- **Feeling:** War room confidence. "I'm in command. Every metric I need is right here. Time to make decisions."
- **Data density:** High. This is a tool for people who live in spreadsheets and ad managers. Don't shy away from information — organize it well.

### Design Tokens (Established)
- **Dark backgrounds:** Warm green-tinted (`#101312` base, `#1A1D1B` surface, `#232724` elevated)
- **Light backgrounds:** Cool slate (`#F8FAFC` base, `#FFFFFF` surface, `#F1F5F9` elevated)
- **Brand blue:** `#334FB4` (primary), `#4A6BD6` (hover/light)
- **Warm gold:** `#EDBF63` (dark mode), `#D97706` (light mode) — used for highlights, important metrics, warnings
- **Neon accent:** `#B8E636` — primary data color (chart lines, area fills, progress bars, positive highlights). Inspired by InsightX analytics aesthetic.
- **Success:** `#34D399` (dark), `#059669` (light) — semantic success, used alongside neon accent
- **Danger:** `#EF4444` (dark), `#DC2626` (light)
- **Typography:** Urbanist (Jordan's pick — geometric, clean, modern). Sofia Pro was originally declared but never loaded. PP Editorial New mentioned for decorative headlines but never implemented.
- **Borders:** Subtle, 1px, theme-aware (`#2A2E2B` dark, `#E2E8F0` light)
- **Radius:** `rounded-md` (6px) for buttons/badges, `rounded-lg` (8px) for cards/panels

### Design Principles
1. **Data is the design.** The numbers, charts, and tables ARE the interface. Don't decorate around them — make them the star. Clear hierarchy, strong typography on values, muted labels.
2. **Density over decoration.** Pack useful information in. A media buyer scanning 50 ads doesn't want whitespace — they want answers fast. But dense doesn't mean chaotic: use spacing rhythm and grouping to organize.
3. **Earn every pixel.** No decorative elements that don't serve a purpose. No sparklines that don't convey trend. No icons that don't aid recognition. If it doesn't help make a decision, cut it.
4. **Dark mode is the real product.** Design for dark first, ensure light works. The war room runs at night.
5. **Familiar patterns, sharp execution.** Media buyers know Triple Whale, Google Ads, Meta Ads Manager. Don't reinvent navigation or metric display patterns — execute the familiar ones with more precision and clarity than the originals.

### Accessibility
Standard best practices — primarily internal tool. No specific WCAG requirements, but maintain readable contrast ratios and don't rely solely on color to convey meaning (use directional arrows alongside green/red).
